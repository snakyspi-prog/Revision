Energy Transfer Pathways

Energy is always conserved — it moves between stores by these pathways:

Mechanically
Forces acting (e.g. pushing, stretching)
Electrically
Current carrying energy round a circuit
By Heating
Conduction, convection, radiation
By Radiation
EM waves (e.g. light, infrared, microwave)

Reducing Thermal Energy Transfers

Ways to Reduce Unwanted Transfer

  • Lubrication — reduces friction between surfaces, less heat wasted
  • Thermal insulation — traps air (poor conductor), reduces conduction/convection
  • Cavity wall insulation — foam filled gap; reduces convection in air gap
  • Double-glazing — air/argon gap between panes insulates
  • Loft insulation — fibreglass traps air, reduces conduction through ceiling
  • Shiny surfaces reflect IR radiation back → less heat lost by radiation

Key Facts — Current, Voltage, Resistance

  • Current = flow of charge (electrons). Measured in Amps (A).
  • Conventional current flows + → −; electrons flow − → +
  • Potential difference = energy transferred per unit charge. Measured in Volts (V).
  • Ohmic conductor — straight I-V graph; constant resistance at constant temp
  • Filament bulb — resistance increases as temperature increases (non-ohmic, curved I-V)
  • Diode — very high resistance in reverse direction; very low resistance forward
  • LDR — resistance DECREASES as light intensity INCREASES (in a sensor circuit)
  • Thermistor — resistance DECREASES as temperature INCREASES
  • Ammeter in series; Voltmeter in parallel across component

Key Facts — Particle Model

  • Latent heat of fusion = energy to melt solid (at constant temperature)
  • Latent heat of vaporisation = energy to boil liquid — LARGER than fusion (more bonds to break)
  • Temperature is proportional to mean kinetic energy of particles
  • Absolute zero (0 K) = minimum possible temperature; particles have minimum KE
  • Gas pressure is caused by particles colliding with container walls
  • ↑ Temperature → particles faster → harder and more frequent collisions → ↑ pressure
  • ↓ Volume → particles hit walls more often → ↑ pressure (same temp)
  • Density of water = 1000 kg/m³; density of ice ≈ 917 kg/m³ (less dense than water — unusual!)
⭐ HIGHER ONLY — Gas Law Graphs: A pV = constant graph is a hyperbola (p vs V). A p vs T (Kelvin) graph is a straight line through the origin when volume is constant. If the line is extrapolated back to p=0, it hits the x-axis at absolute zero (0 K / −273°C).

4.1.3 History of the Atomic Model Triple Physics

Development of the Atomic Model

~1803
DALTON
Solid Sphere Model — Atoms described as tiny, indivisible solid spheres. No internal structure. Elements differ in mass. This explained chemical reactions but had no explanation for electrical properties.
1897
THOMSON
Plum Pudding Model — Discovery of the electron. Thomson proposed a sphere of positive charge with electrons (negative "plums") embedded in it — like plums in a pudding. This explained why atoms were overall neutral.
1909–11
RUTHERFORD
Nuclear/Planetary Model — The famous gold foil (alpha scattering) experiment by Geiger and Marsden, directed by Rutherford. Most alpha particles passed straight through → atom is mostly empty space. A small number deflected at large angles (some bounced back) → a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus exists. Disproved the plum pudding model.
1913
BOHR
Bohr's Model — Electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed energy levels (shells). Electrons can jump to higher levels by absorbing energy (photons), and fall back down by emitting energy (light of specific wavelengths/colours). This explained atomic emission spectra.
1932
CHADWICK
Discovery of the Neutron — Explained why atomic masses were roughly double atomic numbers. Completed the nuclear model we use today: protons + neutrons in nucleus, electrons in shells.
Rutherford Scattering Experiment Triple

What the Results Proved

1
Most pass straight through → the atom is mostly empty space — disproves plum pudding
2
Some deflected at small angles → a positive charge exists inside the atom that repels alpha (also positive)
3
A very few bounced almost straight back → the positive charge is concentrated in a very small, very dense region = the nucleus (less than 1/10,000 of atom's size)

Contamination vs Irradiation

CONTAMINATION

Radioactive material gets onto or inside the body (skin, clothes, ingested, inhaled). The source stays with you — continuous dose. MORE dangerous because you cannot remove yourself from the source.

Most harmful: Alpha — very highly ionising inside body tissue

IRRADIATION

Body is exposed to radiation from an external source. Source remains outside you — dose stops when you move away. LESS dangerous than contamination.

Most harmful externally: Gamma — penetrates to internal organs

✓ Exam answer template: Always say WHICH type is more dangerous in WHICH scenario and WHY (ionising power / penetration).
Half-Life

Half-Life Key Facts & Calculations

  • Half-life = time for the count rate (activity) to halve, OR for half the unstable nuclei to decay
  • Radioactive decay is random and spontaneous — cannot be predicted or controlled
  • Activity measured in Becquerels (Bq) = number of decays per second
  • Count rate measured by Geiger-Müller tube (Geiger counter)
  • After n half-lives: N = N₀ × (½)ⁿ
  • Background radiation must be subtracted from readings!
WORKED EXAMPLE:

Initial activity = 800 Bq. Half-life = 2 days. What is activity after 8 days?

8 days ÷ 2 days = 4 half-lives

800 → 400 → 200 → 100 → 50 Bq

OR: 800 × (½)⁴ = 800 × 1/16 = 50 Bq ✓

Background Radiation

Sources of Background Radiation

🪨
Radon Gas

~50% of background radiation in UK. Emitted naturally from granite rocks. Builds up in poorly-ventilated buildings.

🌌
Cosmic Rays

~13% — high-energy particles from space. More exposure at altitude (pilots, frequent flyers).

🏥
Medical

~15% — X-rays, gamma-ray cancer treatment, medical tracers (e.g. Tc-99m). Artificial source.

🥩
Food & Drink

~12% — small amounts of radioactive isotopes (e.g. K-40, C-14) naturally present in food and water.

🌋
Ground/Buildings

~10% — gamma radiation from naturally radioactive materials in rocks and soil.

☢️
Nuclear Industry

<1% — nuclear power stations and weapons testing fallout contribute very little to average dose.

⚠ EXAM TRAP: Always subtract background radiation count rate from measured count rate before calculations. This is called the "corrected count rate".

REQUIRED

Required Practicals

These practicals appear directly in the exam — know the method, variables, sources of error, and how to improve accuracy.

Practical 1 — Specific Heat Capacity (Topic 1)

Aim: Measure the specific heat capacity of a material (e.g. aluminium block)

Method:

  • Measure mass of block (balance)
  • Insert immersion heater and thermometer into block
  • Measure initial temperature
  • Switch on heater; record energy (joule-meter or P×t) and temperature every minute
  • Plot temperature vs energy graph; gradient = 1/mc → c = 1/(m × gradient)

Key formula: ΔE = mcΔθ → rearrange to c = ΔE/(mΔθ)

Errors: Heat lost to surroundings (insulate block); poor thermal contact (use thermal paste); energy going to heater itself

Practical 2 — IV Characteristics (Topic 2)

Aim: Determine resistance of components and plot I-V graphs

  • Connect component in series with ammeter and variable resistor
  • Connect voltmeter in parallel across component
  • Vary voltage using variable resistor; record V and I
  • Reverse connections to get negative values
  • Plot I (y-axis) vs V (x-axis)

Key formula: R = V/I (gradient of V-I graph)

Errors: Component heats up → resistance changes; take readings quickly; use small currents

Practical 3 — Density of Materials (Topic 3)

Aim: Measure density of regular and irregular solids, and liquids

  • Regular solid: measure dimensions with ruler/calipers → calculate V; mass on balance → ρ = m/V
  • Irregular solid: displace water in measuring cylinder (eureka can); volume = water displaced
  • Liquid: mass empty cylinder, add liquid, measure volume from scale → m(liquid) = m(total) − m(cylinder)

Key formula: ρ = m/V (SI units: kg/m³ or g/cm³)

Errors: Trapped air bubbles; reading meniscus at eye level; not fully submerging irregular solid

Practical 4 — Radioactive Decay Simulation (Topic 4)

Aim: Model radioactive decay using dice (or actual sources with GM tube)

  • Dice method: 100 dice in a bag; shake and remove all showing '6' (decayed); count remaining; repeat and record
  • Plot number remaining vs number of throws
  • Determine half-life (number of throws for N to halve)
  • Real source: use GM tube to count clicks; subtract background count; plot count rate vs time

Key point: Decay is random and spontaneous — individual events cannot be predicted

Errors: Must subtract background radiation; statistical variation with small samples

🎯 Last-Minute Exam Tips

  • Always show your working — method marks even if answer is wrong
  • Always include units in your answer
  • For 6-mark questions, use PEEL: Point → Evidence → Explain → Link
  • Subtract background count rate before ALL radioactivity calculations
  • Kelvin — never use °C in gas law calculations
  • Efficiency is always a number ≤ 1.0 (or ≤ 100%) — if you get >1, you've made an error
  • In series: current SAME everywhere; voltage divides
  • In parallel: voltage SAME in each branch; current divides
  • State changes: temp STAYS CONSTANT — say "energy breaks bonds"
  • Radioactive decay: always say "random AND spontaneous"
  • Nuclear notation: protons = atomic number = electrons (neutral atom)
  • For energy resources: mention reliability, cost, AND environmental impact
